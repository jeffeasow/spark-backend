How to write effective emails in an Indian corporate environment

The most common mistake Indian professionals make in emails is burying
the main point. Most people write three paragraphs of context before
stating what they actually need. Busy managers read the first two lines
and move on. Lead with the ask.

Structure every professional email like this:
1. One line stating what you need or why you are writing
2. Brief context — two to three sentences maximum
3. Clear next step or call to action

Subject lines matter more than most people think. A subject line like
"Update" or "Regarding the project" tells the reader nothing. Be specific.
Write "Approval needed for Q3 budget by Friday" or "Quick question about
onboarding process" — the reader knows exactly what to expect.

Tone in Indian corporate emails often swings too formal or too casual.
Avoid starting with "Respected Sir/Madam" in modern tech and BFSI companies
— it reads as outdated. Also avoid overly casual openers with senior
stakeholders. "Hi [First name]" is appropriate for most Indian corporates
in 2024.

Hedging language weakens your emails. Phrases like "just wanted to check",
"if it's not too much trouble", "sorry to bother you" signal low confidence.
Remove them. Your ask is valid — state it directly.

When writing to senior stakeholders:
- Keep it under 150 words if possible
- State the decision or input you need upfront
- Give them an easy way to respond — a yes/no question is better than
  an open ended one
- Never send a problem without a proposed solution

Follow up etiquette in India:
- Wait 2 business days before following up on a non-urgent email
- One follow up is professional, two starts to feel pushy
- A good follow up line: "Following up on my email below — happy to
  discuss if it's easier than replying"

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Reply all when only one person needs to see it
- Writing in all caps for emphasis — use bold sparingly instead
- Sending late night emails and expecting immediate replies
- CC-ing the boss to apply pressure — this damages relationships
