How to give and receive feedback in Indian workplaces

Giving feedback is one of the hardest skills in Indian corporate culture.
There is a strong cultural tendency to avoid direct criticism to preserve
harmony and respect. This leads to vague feedback that nobody can act on,
and performance issues that fester unaddressed.

The SBI model works well in Indian contexts:
Situation — describe the specific situation
Behaviour — describe the specific behaviour you observed
Impact — describe the impact it had

Example of weak feedback: "You need to be more proactive."
Example of SBI feedback: "In yesterday's client call (Situation), you
waited for the client to ask questions rather than presenting our
recommendations (Behaviour). This made us look underprepared and the
client asked if we had done the analysis (Impact)."

The second version is harder to say but infinitely more useful.

Tips for giving feedback to peers in India:
- Choose a private setting — never give critical feedback in front of others
- Start by acknowledging what is working — not as a formula but genuinely
- Be specific about the behaviour, not the person
- Focus on future change, not past blame
- Check for understanding — ask them to reflect back what they heard

Giving feedback upward (to your manager) in India:
This is genuinely difficult in hierarchical Indian corporate culture.
Most employees avoid it entirely. When you do give upward feedback:
- Frame it as a request, not a complaint
- Use "I" statements: "I find it hard to prioritise when I receive
  tasks from multiple directions — could we align on a weekly priority?"
- Pick the right moment — not when your manager is stressed or rushed
- One specific, actionable point lands better than a list of grievances

Receiving feedback without becoming defensive:
The instinct when receiving criticism is to explain or defend.
Resist this. Instead:
- Listen fully before responding
- Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?"
- Thank the person for the feedback — even if it stings
- Take 24 hours before deciding whether you agree or disagree
- Follow up with what you are going to do differently

Feedback culture in Indian startups vs large IT companies:
Startups tend to have more direct feedback culture.
Large IT companies like TCS, Infosys, Wipro tend to be more hierarchical
and feedback flows mostly top-down. In these environments, building a
relationship of trust with your manager before expecting honest feedback
is important.
