How to grow your career in Indian corporate environments

The most common career mistake Indian professionals make is assuming that
good work alone leads to promotion. It does not. Visibility, relationships,
and strategic communication of your work matter as much as the work itself.

Making your work visible without self-promotion feeling awkward:
Indian professionals often feel uncomfortable talking about their
achievements — it feels like bragging. Reframe it as keeping stakeholders
informed, not self-promotion.

Practical ways to build visibility:
- Send a brief weekly update to your manager on what you accomplished
  and what is coming up — three bullet points is enough
- Volunteer to present in team meetings rather than always being a
  silent contributor
- Write internal posts or share learnings on company communication tools
- Ask to be included in cross-functional projects that give you exposure
  to senior leaders

How promotions actually work in Indian IT and BFSI companies:
Promotions rarely happen automatically. You need to:
1. Know exactly what the next level looks like — ask your manager
   explicitly: "What does strong performance at the next level look like?"
2. Start demonstrating those behaviours before the promotion, not after
3. Have a direct conversation: "I am targeting a promotion in the next
   cycle — what do I need to do between now and then?"
4. Build advocates — your skip-level manager and peers who can vouch
   for your readiness matter

Salary negotiation in India:
Research the market rate using Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, and AmbitionBox.
Know your number before the conversation.

When given an offer or increment:
- Always ask for time to consider — "Can I have 24 hours to review this?"
- Come back with a specific counter, not a vague "I was expecting more"
- Anchor to market data: "Based on my research, the market rate for this
  role in Bengaluru is between X and Y — I was expecting something in
  that range"
- Have a walkaway number in your head before the conversation

Managing your career in a flat organisation vs hierarchical one:
In flat organisations — typically startups — take on cross-functional work,
own outcomes end to end, and build relationships across the company.
In hierarchical organisations — large IT, BFSI — manage your manager well,
understand the political landscape, and build relationships with
decision-makers above your direct manager.

Skills that are consistently undervalued by Indian professionals but
critical for career growth:
- Executive communication — the ability to summarise complex situations
  in 3 sentences for a senior leader
- Stakeholder management — keeping multiple people aligned and informed
- Managing up — understanding what your manager needs and making their
  life easier
- Strategic prioritisation — knowing what NOT to work on
